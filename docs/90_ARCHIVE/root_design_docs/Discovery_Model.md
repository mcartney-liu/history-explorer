# History Explorer - Discovery Model

Version: 1.0

Status: Active

---

# 1. Overview

Discovery Model defines what "discovery" means in History Explorer. It establishes the
product-level discovery model that will guide future exploration experience, guidance,
recommendation strategy, and visualization design.

History Explorer is not built for users to simply search and read historical facts. Its
core value is helping users continuously discover new historical understanding through
connected knowledge.

Discovery is different from reading information. Reading delivers a fact the user already
knew they wanted. Discovery delivers a connection the user did not know existed - a
relationship between two historical entities, a cause they had not considered, a
perspective they had not seen. Discovery is what turns isolated facts into understanding,
and understanding is what keeps the user exploring.

---

# 2. Discovery Philosophy

The discovery experience rests on a few core beliefs.

- **Discovery creates understanding.** A fact is remembered briefly; a connection is
  understood. Discovery is the moment a fact becomes part of a larger picture.

- **Discovery reveals hidden connections.** History is full of links that are not obvious
  until someone points them out. Discovery surfaces those links.

- **Discovery encourages curiosity.** Every discovery raises a new question. The more the
  user discovers, the more they want to keep going.

- **Discovery expands historical perspective.** By seeing events from more angles and more
  connections, the user builds a wider and more accurate view of the past.

These beliefs extend the exploration loop from Exploration_Path.md, the decision
principles from Exploration_Strategy.md, and the movement model from Navigation_Model.md
into the moment where understanding actually forms.

# 3. Types of Discovery

Discovery can take several forms. Each reveals a different kind of historical value.

## Connection Discovery

Discovering relationships between historical entities.

Value: Shows how two seemingly separate parts of history are bound together, turning
isolation into context.

## Timeline Discovery

Finding connections across different time periods.

Value: Reveals how an earlier event shaped a later one, or how patterns repeat across
eras, giving the user a sense of historical continuity.

## Civilization Discovery

Understanding interactions between civilizations.

Value: Shows how societies influenced, conflicted with, or succeeded one another, placing
each civilization inside a larger human story.

## Cause and Effect Discovery

Discovering historical causes and consequences.

Value: Explains why events happened and what they led to, converting a list of events into
a chain of causation.

## Perspective Discovery

Viewing historical events from different perspectives.

Value: Challenges a single telling of history by showing the same event through the eyes
of different participants, cultures, or time periods.

Together these types ensure that discovery is not one experience but many, each deepening
understanding in a different way.

---

# 4. Discovery Journey

A typical discovery moves through the following stages.

Question

↓

Exploration

↓

Relationship

↓

New Understanding

↓

New Question

The user begins with a question, explores to find material, encounters a relationship
between entities, gains new understanding, and that understanding immediately raises a new
question. The journey is circular: a discovery does not end exploration, it feeds the next
one. This is why every discovery naturally leads to further exploration - the product
should make the next question easy to follow.

---

# 5. Discovery Principles

The discovery experience follows these product-level principles.

- **Encourage unexpected discoveries.** Beyond what the user expects, surface connections
  that surprise and inform.

- **Maintain historical context.** A discovery should always be shown inside the
  relationships that make it meaningful, never as a bare fact.

- **Connect isolated knowledge.** The highest-value discoveries join things the user had
  kept separate.

- **Reward curiosity.** Every question the user asks should be met with a discovery that
  makes the asking worthwhile.

- **Support continuous exploration.** Discovery is a step in a longer journey, not a final
  answer.

These principles align with the exploration principles in Exploration_Strategy.md and the
navigation principles in Navigation_Model.md.

---

# 6. Measuring Discovery

Discovery success may be evaluated at a product level. These are product-level intentions,
not analytics specifications or technical metrics.

- **Number of meaningful exploration paths.** How many real next steps a discovery opens.

- **Depth of exploration.** How far the user continues after a discovery, rather than
  stopping.

- **Diversity of connected topics.** How varied the entities and relationships a user
  reaches through discovery.

- **Continued exploration after a discovery.** Whether a discovery leads to another
  question and another exploration, rather than an endpoint.

The intent is to treat discovery as a continuing experience: a good discovery is one that
leads somewhere, not one that closes the book.

---

# 7. Future Expansion

Future versions of History Explorer may build on this discovery model. These are
possibilities, not commitments, and no implementation is described.

- **AI-assisted discovery.** Helping the user notice connections they might miss.

- **Personalized discovery.** Surfacing discoveries matched to what the user has explored.

- **Collaborative discovery.** Letting users share and build on each other's discoveries.

- **Knowledge Graph discovery.** Revealing discoveries as a navigable graph of connected
  entities.

- **Cross-civilization exploration.** Discovering links that span different civilizations
  and cultures.
